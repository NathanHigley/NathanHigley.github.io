Welcome

A Push For Freedom Out of Earth

 The Space Race is a milestone of achievement, for the United States, the Soviet Union, and mankind. Ambition, innovation, and cooperation come to a head in America, as they fight their way to the stars to safeguard their founding principle:


Freedom.

Origins

The Arms Race

 The 1950s marked the beginning of the United States and Soviet Union's Cold War in the aftermath of the second World War. Events such as the Cuban Missile Crisis and the Berlin Wall construction would spur tension and a subsequent arms race between the two new superpowers, competing for global dominance. Unbenownst to America, the Soviets instead turned their gaze to conquer the heavens.

The Race Begins

Sputnik, The Traveler

 In 1957, the Soviets launched an artificial satellite into space, the Sputnik. This would mark the first man-made object placed into Earth's orbit. A surprise most unpleasant, America, too, turned to the stars, in the fear of the military and intelligence dominance the Soviets could establish in space. The National Aeronautics and Space Administration was organized, and the US began to seek to pioneer the final frontier first.

The First Cosmonauts

Yuri Gagarin and Alan Shepard, The First Men

 In 1961, the Soviets would send Yuri Gagarin into space aboard the Vostok 1. He would successfully orbit the Earth before his return. In response, NASA dubbed their effort "Project Mercury," and put Alan Shepard in space just a few months after the Soviets.

We Choose

Not Because They're Easy, But Because They're Hard

  That same year, President John F. Kennedy would make a grand speech to the public, claiming the US would put a man on the moon before the turn of the decade. As famously quoted, "We choose to go to the Moon! We choose to go to the Moon in this decade and do the other things, not because they are easy, but because they are hard; because that goal will serve to organize and measure the best of our energies and skills, because that challenge is one that we are willing to accept, one we are unwilling to postpone, and one we intend to win, and the others, too." Within a year, NASA's lunar mission "Project Apollo" would initiate.

To Go To The Moon

One Small Step

 Through the 1960s, NASA would operate with immensely increased budget and staffing. Despite fatal disasters and countless setbacks, the United States would attempt their first lunar landing in 1969. Aboard Apollo 11, Neil Armstrong, Edwin Aldrin, and Michael Collins successfully landed and walked on the moon's surface.

Legacy

One Giant Leap

 With the moon successfully conquered, the Space Race came to a close. Astronauts would become celebrities, and the boundless possiblity that space provided brought American culture into a frenzy for decades to come. Despite the Soviets being absolutely villainized by their opposing role in the race, relations would actually improve through joint missions in space.


Ultimately, America's drive and aspiration for their country would bring them to a victory, one that would benefit mankind as a whole, and the freedom of the future.
